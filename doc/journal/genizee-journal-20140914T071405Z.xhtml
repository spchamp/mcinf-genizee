Relevance of Phase Angle

A practical relevance of capacitive reactance and inductive reactance may be observed for their relation towards a determination of inductance and capacitance quantities in an RLC circuit, such as towards ensuring that an RLC circuit would function with the inductive and capacitive reactances "in phase." Certainly, the frequency of the power supply in an AC circuit would be of some relevance, too. The hypothetical RLC AC circuit could be designed such that the inductance and capacitance values - whether in a series RLC circuit or a parallel RLC circuit - in effect, that the quantities of net capacitance and net inductance, in the AC RLC circuit, together with the frequency of the circuit's power supply, would summarily arrive the circuit to being "in phase", as between the inductive reactance and capacitive reactance, in magnitude and phase angle of each.

Of course, such calculation would entail a few procedural steps, as towards a calculation of the appropriate inductance, capacitance, and specific power source frequency for the respective RLC circuit.

Certainly, circuit impedance has a relevance, also. 

Phase Angle in Inductive Reactance and Capacitive Reactance

Considering, broadly, the following features of an RC, RL, or RLC Circuit:

  • The phase difference between source voltage and circuit current, specifically in RL and RLC circuits - and relatedly, Inductive Reactance
  • The phase difference in RC and RLC circuits - and relatedly, Capacitive Reactance in the circuit

In a circuit using an AC power supply – whether a circuit composed as an RC, RL, or RLC circuit - both of capacitive reactance and inductive reactance contribute to net circuit impedance.

A Relevance of Impedance

In an AC circuit, circuit impedance is relevant for calculation of circuit voltage and current, in a manner analogous to Ohm's Law - noting that Ohm's Law may be applied for calculation of voltage and current over constant or variable net resistance, in a DC circuit, as with the mathematical relations between circuit current, voltage, and resistance denoted in Ohm's Law.

As modeled in the formulas I=V/Z and V=IZ, impedance 'Z' assumes an inverse relation to circuit current and a direct relation to circuit voltage, respectively, within an AC circuit -- perhaps in a manner analogous to net resistance, in a resistance only circuit, whether of a DC or AC circuit. Therefore, electrical impedance would demonstrate a fundamental role in analysis of a existing AC circuits, if not furthermore in design of new AC circuits.

Considering the interaction of inductive reactance and capacitive reactance in an RLC circuit, certainly both types of electrical reactance - such as may be calculated to a net impedance, in an RC, RL, or RLC circuit, as whether of a series or parallel circuit, in each  - but primarily in regards to RLC circuits, both capacitive reactance and inductive reactance may find a particular relevance for their geometric and mechanical relation to circuit impedance and, inversely, circuit admittance in RLC AC circuits.

Determination of L, C, and Resonance Frequency in Design of RLC Circuits

Transitively, both of inductive reactance and capacitive reactance - as would be related to electrical impedance in RLC circuits - together, would be of relevance as towards the calculation of a total inductance and of a total capacitance such that would be required, in an AC circuit design, for the circuit to arrive at a state of being in "resonance" between capacitive reactance and inductive reactance, in the circuit. In a practical sense, certainly an RLC AC circuit may be designed such that the net capacitive reactance and net inductive reactance would each be of a quantity as to serve to ensure that the circuit, on its power supply, would be "at resonance," in the circuit's normal functioning?

Of course, the AC frequency provided of the electrical power supply might be assumed to provide a constant quantity to the same calculations - assuming no significant variance in the AC power supply's frequency and peak voltage.

As contrasted to the simpler calculation of resonance frequency in a series RLC circuit, the relation of capacitive reactance, inductive reactance, and circuit frequency in the calculation of resonance frequency in a parallel RLC circuit may be defined in a number of methods. Perhaps, the method that would be of the most practical relevance -- among the alternatives for such calculation -- perhaps would be the calculation of the "In phase" resonance frequency, as would be calculated of the parallel RLC circuit's resistance, reactance, and qualities of the circuit's power supply.
